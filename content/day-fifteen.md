...both in terms of time (15 out of 28 days) and distance (over 5000 out of around 9000 miles traveled). It's yet another reminder that this trip won't last forever, and that staying for so little time in each spot lets us part before we get too attached. It's bittersweet, but perhaps getting a mere taste of each city is what allows the visit to be all the more inspiring. Each stop is a small cliffhanger, and hopefully I'll be able to resolve each one before too long.

Anyway, today was our full day in **Portland**, city of microbrews, bars, strip clubs, food, and nature. As per the recommendation of an Uber driver from last night, and corroborated by everyone else we asked, we decided to hit up a place called [Screen Door](http://screendoorrestaurant.com/), which allegedly has the best brunch ever. The rumors weren't really wrong - everything was pretty top notch and in huge portions... the only problem is that it's all Louisiana style! I did my best not to unfairly compare the dishes to what I ate a mere week and a half ago. My worries, thoug, were unfounded, as everything seemed to live up to the competition.

After we ate just enough to go from satisfied to self-hatred, we bounced and ventured over to [Powell's City of Books](http://www.powells.com/locations/powells-city-of-books), the flagship location of the indepedent bookstore chain. Whereas the Strand is a bit of an institution in Manhattan, this bookstore is an annexed haven for literature without the claustrophobia of being in New York City. The store is seriously massive, and every shelf has a great deal on a lightly used book for a killer price. I ended up leaving with a hardcover copy of The Math Book, and another on some computer science theory. Aron got a bunch of children's books, including a copy of Good Night Moon and the 11th hour. I wonder if this says something about our academic aptitude (it doesn't).

We also ventured up into the rare books room, which was the one area where The Strand wins out for sure. Still, it was really cool seeing a first edition 1984 for 500 dollars. Perhaps just as cool as the bookstore itself, I saw a guy sitting outside with a typewriter and a jar of colored pieces of paper with a pretty welcoming smile on his face, and a sign that denoted an offer for a custom poem - given a prompt. Aron and I had seen a few of these arists in NOLA some time prior, but they were all really busy with other people's creations, and I never got the chance to get one for myself. I told him briefly that I was on a trip, around the country, and that I'd like a poem now that I was over halfway done and heading eastward towards home.

At this point, the bloat of the massive meal we had this morning started to set in, so we grabbed a quick ride up to the top of [Pittock Mansion](http://pittockmansion.org/) - one of the historic sights in the city. The best part, by far, was the excellent view of the city from high up (picture at the top), as well as the miles long trails that are woven through Forest Park. It's quite beautiful, and it was a really easy walk. We ventured out to the rose gardens, but they were mostly closed, as there are few roses to show off at the end of October. A shame, but a nice walk nonetheless.

I was really afraid that walking a whopping three miles would have burned too many calories from this morning, so we took a trip down to the very iconic (and allegedly overrated) [Voodoo Doughnut](http://voodoodoughnut.com/) shop. The shop is a bit touristy, and sometimes has some awful lines, but it's a must-do at least once if in Portland. Luckily for us, few people were looking for hefty doughnuts at five in the afternoon on a Wednesday. I grabbed a box of their most popular offerings, and we brought it back to the hotel room for eating while Aron and I watched the debate. The debate went about as expected.
