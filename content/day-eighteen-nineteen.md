I know I've complained a lot in these posts about the brevity of each stop, and expressed a desire to come back to spend more time. It's undoubtedly, to at least some extent, driven by some mixture of guilt and wonder. The trip was always meant to just be an endless string of teasing stops, but the intent was that I would be moving on before getting too attached to any one place along my visit. With the exception of Vegas, I haven't truly been to any of these locations that I've written about before - so I honestly had no intention of becoming so enamored with any particular stop. Then we went through Yellowstone.

Then we stayed in Cody.

Very briefly last time, I had mentioned that I was staying at [Apple Jack Ranch](http://applejackranch.com/), a place that I had found through AirBnB. I was tired, and still taking everything in from Yellowstone when I had written the previous post on my cell phone. I didn't really quite notice that I had struck gold in terms of lodging choices. When we arrived, everything was pitch black, and I was really looking forward to seeing some of the last moments of the Orionids light show in the night sky (it was a fruitful endeavor). Exhausted and blog post written (and committed to the repo, and deployed, all from an android ssh session - what a pain), I passed right out.

I woke up to the sound of paws prodding the deck just outside the door. In my six thirty in the morning delirium, I ran through the various possibilities. Perhaps, it was one of the many rabbits we saw scurrying away from the car as we approached the cabin the night before. Perhaps it's the owner's dog, which I had vaguely remembered existing. But this is Wyoming, of course. It could be something a great deal more feral. Maybe even a grizzly (don't laugh - apparently last year, one approached our very cabin)! Being the brave soul I am, I creaked open the door just a tad, and in the darkness of morning I saw something somewhat large, on all fours, looking right back at me for a moment before hopping off the porch. Out of instinct, I of course closed the door again immediately to contemplate what I had just saw. It was this guy.

Certain that being maimed wasn't likely on the immediately list of accomplishments for the morning, I opened the door once more, and saw the big canine staring back at me on the dirt road. I approached him (leaving Aron behind to sleep in, as per usual), and he padded onwards. Any time he got a bit ahead, he looked back. A short walk up the road led me to a side door at precisely seven in the morning, right about at first light. I opened up to the smell of coffee, and smiling faces. Less than a minute later, we were all deep into conversation regarding travel and more. Around an hour later, everyone put their mugs away, since it was time to do some work - bringing in the horses so that their vet (a family friend of theirs) could check in on their breeding progress. I was about to excuse myself, but minutes later, I found myself keeping Johanna and Bounty company as they fetched their small group.

Throughout the morning, I caught myself slipping into the mentality of an inquisitive child, asking a whole slew of questions, and watching the pair work. And while I'm somewhat sad to say I didn't really offer anything in terms of help - I didn't get in the way, either! The fact that they humored my curiosity was a delight on its own. Over the course of the weekend, plenty of time was spent just gazing over the ranch, at the mountains in the distance, or at the stars in the sky.

The rest of the afternoon, Aron and I spent mostly in town. Interestingly enough, we had stopped in at a museum called the [Buffalo Bill Center of the West](http://www.centerofthewest.org/). I wasn't really sold on the museum - we had passed by it while driving through the town on the way the previous night - but it came with a pretty strong endorsement from Bounty, so we stopped in. It's easily one of my favorite museums I've ever stepped into (it's a Smithsonian affiliate) - and it's massive, with five sections which are each basically a smaller museum on their own.

Similar to Meow Wolf, time just slipped away from us because it was legitimately very interesting, and much more engaging than most other museums I've ever been to. At some point, there were a few falconers-of-a-fashion who brought in some of the native birds to show off and talk about.

We arrived back on the ranch near sunset, and spent time in the company of our hosts (their little boy, too), and the aforementioned vet friends, near a cozy fire pit.

The following morning was welcoming and familiar to the one before. This time, Rooster (the dog) and I decided to have a race up to the house. Rooster won, but just barely. More coffee, more conversation, and a home cooked breakfast later, it was time to go. In writing all this, it almost seems as though I give the impression that not much happened, but it's really just the opposite. Time felt suspended, if for a brief moment in the trip, and I felt the most relaxed I had ever been in years. At one point during my stay, I had asked Bounty and Jo if the sights of the mountains, and the lifestyle they chose become "normal" or "just another day" for them. They looked content when they told me no. I'm sure I'm glossing over many little things, but I'll be coming back to Apple Jack Ranch - and Yellowstone - for a bit of a longer stay next year.
