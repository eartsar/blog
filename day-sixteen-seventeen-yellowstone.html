Day Sixteen & Seventeen (Yellowstone)

Posted on Sat 22 October 2016 in Eitan Posts

The beauty of the northwest United States can't really be overstated...

A photo posted by Eitan Romanoff (@eitanromanoff) on Oct 21, 2016 at 9:19pm PDT

...and my subpar pictures don't really do it justice. New Orleans was a city I was the most excited to see. Seeing all my friends in Vegas was an event I was looking forward to the most. The Grand Canyon was the sight I was most excited to view. When it comes to the general portion of the trip, however, Yellowstone is perhaps the part I was most excited for since I started planning.

As anyone reading this is fully aware, I'm not the most outdoorsy guy. I code a lot, and read a lot. I'm okay with staying indoors. This trip is a huge step out of my comfort zone - not that I'm afraid to travel, just the opposite, and am quite good at getting around unfamiliar areas - but the act of abandoning familiarity is one I have to really convince myself to do. This portion of the trip, Yellowstone and Wyoming in general (Cody is such a fantastic little town), really epitomizes this departure from the familiar. The culture here is one of respect for the wild, self sufficiency, and pleasure in solitude - as our AirBnB hosts put it - and it's one that I'm growing quite fond of.

We share the park

Aron and I left Portland a bit late, and had a very long and arduous drive ahead. The first few hours were rainy, the middle few hours were empty, and the last few hours were pitch black. Thursday was uniquely a travel day, and we didn't really see much outside of our route. It's a bit of a shame... we missed some great waterfalls outside Portland (the visibility would have been near zero anyway with the weather), and didn't take into account jumping ahead one hour - and our projection to reach Twin Falls at sunset more than just a bit off. Needless to say, when we arrived in Rigby, Idaho, we were in no mood to do much at all besides sleep. Aron, in particular, was quite finished with my company, and probably with good reason.

Gorge in Idaho

A photo posted by Eitan Romanoff (@eitanromanoff) on Oct 21, 2016 at 8:35pm PDT

It was really good, maybe necessary even, that Friday was the single greatest day of the trip. We departed early, and drove about two hours to get to Yellowstone at around half past nine. Yellowstone, at this point in the year, is in the final stages of winding down services for closure during the winter season. All of the hotels and inns are basically closed within the park, and many places outside the park also close due to the slow business. The towns are relatively empty, there are no lines of cars in the park. There are fewer rangers (I think), and no in-park facilities beyond toilets, a year-long medical hub, some ranger stations, and a couple of self-serve unattended gas stations. Of course, it's not completely empty, but the park has only a fraction of its normal volume of tourists. It's amazing.

Having entered the park from the western gate (via West Yellowstone), we snaked our way to all of the major landmarks. We saw the thermal pools, including the Grand Prismatic Spring, on the way to the Old Faithful geyser. All of these sights were a really cool reminder that Yellowstone is a bit of an anomaly, as far as national parks go, as it sits on top of an active volcanic area that enables its many unique features. It's got several flavors of habitat in one region that can all be visited in one day (if you show up when we did), each one beautiful in its own right. Old Faithful was maybe the most overrated thing in the park, but even still, it was really impressive. I would say that all of the thermal sights are more interesting scientifically than they are inspiring. I'm quite happy that we started off with this portion of the trip, as I'm not sure we would have been as attentive to these sights later in the day.

Afterwards, we looped around to West Thumb, which is a point on the large lake that lives in the southeastern quadrant of the park. There were some really magnificent views along the way, and having spent a few hours at this point, we were getting kind of hungry. I had picked up PB&J ingredients earlier, being the gourmet chef that I am, and we found a quiet spot by the lake to make our lunch. A tiny drop of jelly fell to the ground, and the birds descended upon it almost immediately. They jelly. This stop resulted in the only food picture I'll ever be proud of in my entire life (because food pictures are stupid, and if you asked me to take them, you're a horrible person).

After that was the Grand Canyon of Yellowstone, which many people say rivals the Grand Canyon in Arizona for its various sights, rather than scale. It's a compelling argument. Our spot of choice was near the Lower Falls, a mere 5/8 mile walk downhill to the brink of the falls (and a painfully steep 5/8 mile back up later...), which leads to an excellent vantage point. On the way back up, I was asked several times "is it worth it", to which I mostly just grinned like an idiot.

A photo posted by Eitan Romanoff (@eitanromanoff) on Oct 21, 2016 at 9:17pm PDT

From there, we went west, and looped around the northwest quadrant of the park, which houses Mammoth Springs. The stop here was brief, not because we didn't want to see the springs, but because these sections were a filled with larger wildlife that we could see up close (as close as possible without committing a crime - I made sure we weren't "those guys"). Given our general drought of wildlife up to this point, we jumped on the opportunity to observe these beasts up close. Unfortunately, we didn’t see any moose (they’re smaller than their Vermont cousins, I believe) or bears.

I'm glossing over plenty of things we saw along the way in the park, but the complete map has endless vistas for viewing, and trails to hike. You couldn't spend a month here and do everything, though if you show up in the last week of October, you can do all the "iconic" things in a day or two.

We left the park at sunset through the northeastern gate, and poor Aron drove us an hour and a half to Cody on the windy scenic highway. It was a rough drive, but was all worth it in the end as we let the sights of Yellowstone sink in. I can't do it justice with just some crappy smartphone pictures and a rambly blog post, so I'll put it this way... Along with the Grand Canyon, Yellowstone Park is something that every American should see in their lifetime. People find this country endearing for different reasons, but I'd say that these sights make up the most compelling reason of all. I owe it to myself to come back (likely many times) in the future.

I woke up early to excellent conversation with my Cody, WY AirBnB hosts (here's a link to the listing, HIGHLY RECOMMEND IT), and then wrangled some horses with them. We'll be seeing a bunch of stuff here in Cody (and maybe making the hour trek back to Yellowstone, if Aron ever wakes up), so I'll have more to post on this beautiful corner of the country tomorrow.

Until then, there are plenty of other pictures, as per usual, at my Instagram. Though, you can just as easily book a flight to come out here yourself.
